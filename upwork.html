Overview

Upwork is a global freelancing platform where businesses and independent professionals(freelancers) connect and collaborate remotely. Formerly known as Elance-oDesk, Upwork has three million jobs posted annually, worth a total of $1 billion, making it one of the largest freelancing marketplaces.

During the course of this project, I explored the usability of Upwork's desktop site compared to that of its mobile app. Upwork also has a desktop application to track time and communicate(messaging) with clients.

Problem Statement

To get started, It was important to understand the 2 primary users:
1. Clients - looking for qualified people for a job
2. Freelancers - willing to get the job done

Upwork establishes a communication by connecting qualified freelancers to clients.

After launching the app, users have access to a clearly visible navigation bar at the bottom of the screen with five main categories. Considering upwork's motto and areas of concentration, the app is not well tailored to a user’s preferences. Once the user opens the mobile app, he/she gets to see the menu items "Messages, Jobs, Offers, Invitations and others" lined up on the bottom navigation bar. As one continues to use the mobile app, the “Search jobs” tab will be the most used with Messages coming second. The other 2 menu items "Offers and Invitations" are clearly not so frequently used. So, The menu items in the bottom navigation are not so well-organized. Also, Upwork clearly lacks "User control and Freedom" by not allowing users to view/edit profile/settings. Yet, The app makes use of culturally recognized signifiers to create positive, intuitive mappings, familiarity and consistency for users.

Feature analysis

Upwork's mobile apps on both iOS and Android have only a subset of features compared to that on the website. Clearly, the app is focused on achieving only a few taks. Since we are testing for usability, It was important to make sure the test is fair between them. So I performed a comparative analysis of features between Web, iOS and Android versions, and selected only a few tasks that were commonly available on all of them.



Based on the comparative analysis above and a preliminary research on the most common tasks that users perform on upwork, I created a list of following tasks to be evaluated for usability on the mobile app.

1. Search for Jobs and Apply
2. Propose terms and conditions
3. Accept/Decline Offers and Invitations
4. Messaging between clients and freelancers

A total of 5 users(who have used upwork website before) tested out both the website and the app for the 4 tasks identified from prior evaluation.

Major findings

1. INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE - The heirarchy of menu items was clearly not so well-organized


2. USER FREEDOM - There was no option to set availability or view/edit profile settings



3. NO FILTERS FOR SEARCH - The website has an overwhelming amount of filters, whereas the mobile app has none

1. Customers are unhappy with the food they receive

Existing design allows users to set preferences like Vegan or Vegetarian. This could result in customers getting food they may not like.
Example 1. Customer who doesn't like Sushi, gets Sushi in their mystery box.
Example 2. Customer gets food with ingredents they are allergic to(Peanut allergy).

2. Customers are unaware of their order status

Existing design doesn't let users track their food. This could result in food left at the user's doorstep. If not notified, it could remain there for hours.

3. "Change of plans" for the customer

Due to unforseen reasons, if the customer needs to change his plans for the evening. The current design doesn't let subscribers "skip" delivery for that day. This could result in more food wastage and loss for the subscriber.
Example: The customer gets invited to a birthday party for the evening and he/she needs to cancel delivery for that day
